Throughout our partnerships, we focus on the most critical issues

affecting the vulnerable in SADC

High Poverty

50% of the population live on less than 1$ a day. Economic growth will be key to addressing this.

Limited Finance

MSMEs have limited access to finance for investment, trade and working capital, limiting their ability to employ people and grow the economy.

High Exclusion

43% of citizens are fully excluded from the formal financial system, limiting their ability to send money to loved ones, or access finance for things they need.

Climate Change

The majority of SADC nations are at risk of climate disasters and green growth will be crucial if the region is to be resilient and sustainable in the future.

COVID-19

Every SADC state has experienced a contracted economy – with the livelihoods of women hit hardest. To build back better, will need appropriate support services – including financial services, G2P and more.

Lack of Innovation

Low adoption of mobile money and other innovations in SADC means the last mile of financial services delivery – and first point of entry for many customers – is blocked.

Our 6 Intervention areas

are closely linked to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

MSME and Agriculture Interventions

Catalytic interventions in MSME and agriculture to create jobs especially for women and youth, through:

‣ SADC Supplier Development programme to build MSMEs to support local and eventual global markets.

‣ High potential value chains & MSME sector projects, particularly in agriculture.

‣ Digital platforms and fintech support linkages and access for key target markets – such as women and youth.

‣ Policy and market catalytic actions and linkages to ensure the market keeps supporting MSMEs and agricultural value chain participants

Green Interventions and inclusive economies

Investment for a greener, inclusive COVID-19 recovery in the SADC region by supporting

‣ Access to finance for investment, trade and working capital for MSMEs to come back greener

‣ Support to the SADC policy and investment climate environment through the Support to the Business and Investment Environment regional programme

‣ Green capital market development and reform support for national governments in the SADC region

‣ Attracting social investment capital geared towards positive nvironmental impacts

Regional Trade

Enabling regional trade, to deepen markets and support economic development by supporting:

‣ Regional financial integration platforms through the SIBE programme by supporting the alignment across country

‣ Cross-border remittances to support women informal cross border traders (ICBTs) by leveraging existing digital infrastructure more broadly and effectively

‣ Inclusion of SADC low-value traders in African Continental Free Trade Area arrangements

‣ Trade finance linked to our MSME and value chain activities, leveraging the competitive advantage of different SADC nations by deepening the regional market they can sell to

Digital Finance

Inclusive Digital Finance to promote inclusivity for informal and low-income workers and consumers, and to stimulate local economic development

‣ Supportive regulatory environment (including SADC mobile money guidelines, consumer protection)

‣ Scalable digital solutions for access to insurance, health, and (clean) energy

‣ Community digitisation projects to promote local economic development

‣ Inclusion of informal and low-income workers in the digital economy

‣ Policy level support for digitisation of services for low-income and informal market

Social Protection Systems

Strengthening Social Protection Systems to empower their recipients:

‣ By Partnership with South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) on access to and usage of social grants

‣ Through efficiency of grant payment systems across SADC, pathways out of poverty and financial literacy and capability

‣ Supporting the development of long-term digitisation social protection payments eco-systems

Remittances

Developing cross-border remittances environments to benefit low-value remitters and women beneficiaries by supporting:

‣ Additional corridors, products and cost reductions towards the G20 target of 5% of the remitted value

‣ Expansion of risk-based AML/CFT approaches for poor migrants to access formal services

‣ Digital financial identity, to support onboarding of low value and undocumented customers

About FinMark Trust

(a non-profit organisation)


We are passionate about Africa – and are a vehicle for African professionals who are excited about financial and economic inclusion in the SADC region. Our staff, therefore, reflects a diverse range of Africans, who have deep-rooted ambitions and commitment to ensure a financially inclusive region.

Our Board Our Staff

FMT's mission of making financial markets work for the poor extends to ensuring economic inclusivity and linking financial inclusion to the real economy.

FMT is a non-profit trust fighting poverty and inequality in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) region by ensuring that the poor are part of the economy. We do this by ensuring that financial sector development supports the inclusion of all the people of SADC – specifically by promoting financial inclusion in support of inclusive growth. For 20 years, our work has created deep and sustainable impacts in the lives of the poor, achieved through our partnerships with regional authorities, national governments and the private sector. Our evidence-based approach is key to identifying and addressing the systemic constraints that prevent financial markets from reaching out to unserved and underserved people.



Our capabilities

Through our unique approach, we are able to bring real change and benefits to people in SADC. This approach includes our considerable experience and capacity to “pilot” and implement new ideas in the market, for example our hugely successful remittances product on the Lesotho / Eswatini / SA borders, ongoing digital financial identity pilots, and the introduction of Risk based approaches with financial sector players.

By providing the evidence base to identify the need and blockages, and our technical skills in various areas we are able to help market players formulate suitable products or processes. We test these in the real live market in partnership, and then allow the private sector to roll them out at market scale in a sustainable way.

Our strategic capabilities include the following:

Deep local presence and reach in the SADC region built over the last 20 years that ensures our work is responsive to local needs, a ready access to stakeholders and policy makers across the region, physical presence at SADC Secretariat, an ongoing long term technical support partnership with the SADC Committee of Central Bank Governors (CCBG), 6 country offices within SADC, and ongoing programme activities in multiple SADC countries

Deep relationships across public and private sector, including existing convening and engagement platforms such as the annual regional financial inclusion forum.

Capacity to implement big/ substantial regional projects, backed up by a strong programme, data and operational capacity.

Trusted technical and implementation partner for the SADC Secretariat, CCBG and other SADC sub structures and sub committees, the National Treasury of South Africa, the South African Social Security Agency, and multiple Ministries of Finance and Central banks across the region.

An unrelenting focus on the poor, especially poor women and youth whom our programmes disproportionately benefit.

Our FI journey

Our FI journey

In our journey to impact the lives of the poor many lessons have been learnt. It is clear that financial inclusion is part of a larger development agenda that ultimately results in improved livelihoods. As such, our focus is evolving towards more direct real economy impacts. The new approach named financial inclusion 2.0 is closely tied to the UN sustainable development goals, and to COVID-19 economic recovery.

Women have traditionally enjoyed a high priority in FMT programmes, but this has been further heightened by COVID-19 which research shows has disproportionately affected women. Women-Led enterprises have borne a heavier burden compared to other MSMEs, especially Informal Cross Border Traders who are 70% women and typically are not included in government mitigation plans.
